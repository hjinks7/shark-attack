# Shark Attack Risk Analysis — Executive Summary

**What I did:** Analyzed approximately 6000 reported shark incident over almost 2 century time span. Cleaned up an unorganized public dataset, normalized by population (to separate actual signal for risk vs. statistically generated noise).

**Why it matters:** Headlines about shark attack statistics are often misleading. Small countries may appear as "high risk" due to their relatively low total population. Rarely encountered species will appear extremely risky simply based on limited encounters. The purpose of my research was to identify what is true and what is merely an artifact of the way the data has been collected.

---

## Key Findings

| Finding | Takeaway |
|---|---|
| Great Whites, Tigers & Bulls are high-risk sharks | All three of these species have approximately 20-25% fatality rates when viewed using large sample sizes. They are therefore considered statistically reliable indicators of "high-risk" species that can be used as reference points for public education. |
| Wobbegongs & Coppers show erratic patterns | Because both Wobbegongs and Coppers appear to exhibit highly variable fatality rates (from 0% to extremely high), neither should be relied upon to inform public messaging regarding potential shark risks associated with either of these species; it could result in unnecessary fear-mongering. |
| Fatality rates have declined over time | Based on my analysis, global fatality rates have declined significantly since 1865 (50%+ → ~10–13%). It's likely that improved emergency medical treatment and increased awareness and communication surrounding reported attacks contributed greatly to this trend. As such, declining fatality rates may reflect advances in emergency response rather than reductions in aggressive behavior among sharks.|
| A potential spike in attacks appears from 2021-2026 | It is unclear at this time whether recent spikes in reported attacks may indicate a higher level of actual risk or if it is simply a reflection of increased remote, unmonitored water activities during the pandemic era. |
| Small island nations show extremely high per-capita attack rates | While certain small island nations present themselves as being "at-risk" for shark attacks (due to extremely high per-capita attack rates), virtually all of this apparent danger stems from the "small-denominator" effect rather than indicating greater levels of danger relative to other popular tourist destinations. |
| Some country/decade combinations show real elevated risk even after adjusting for sample size | Rather than focusing on destinations presenting themselves as being at-risk through high-per-capita attack rates, targeted safety investments (such as patrols, nets and enhanced emergency response capabilities) should focus on the country/decade combinations showing elevated adjusted risk. |

---

## Recommendations

1. **Don't rank the risk of specific countries based on unadjusted rates of attacks per capita.** Instead use the adjusted (sample size corrected) or Bayesian-weighted rates to avoid distortions related to population size.
2. **Allocate resources (e.g. patrols, netting, emergency responses, etc.) to locations with elevated adjusted risk, and not merely those with the highest raw number of reported attacks.**
3. **Don't declare a surge in attacks during the 2020s until you have evidence thatt eliminates other possible causes.**
4. **When communicating public risk information, base statements on data pertaining to common species (Great White, Tiger, and Bull), and do not include data regarding rarer species.**
